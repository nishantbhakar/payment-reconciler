# Brainstorming Thread — Payment Reconciliation Tool

---

**Me:** I have a payments reconciliation problem. A platform records transactions instantly when customers pay, but the bank batches and settles funds 1-2 days later. At month end, every transaction should have a matching settlement but they don't always match. I need to build something that finds where the gaps are.

**AI:** Interesting problem. Let me think about what kinds of mismatches you'd actually see in a real payments system:

1. **Timing mismatches** — A transaction happens on Jan 31st but the bank doesn't settle it until Feb 1st or 2nd. At month-end close, it looks like money is missing.
2. **Rounding errors** — Individual transactions might round fine, but when you sum hundreds of $19.99 charges with processing fees, the totals drift by a few cents.
3. **Duplicate entries** — Maybe the platform logs a retry as a second transaction, or the bank posts the same settlement twice.
4. **Orphan records** — A refund appears in the bank feed but there's no original transaction it maps back to. Could be a chargeback processed externally.

**Me:** Yeah those are exactly the scenarios I'm thinking about. How should I structure the data?

**AI:** I'd keep it simple — two CSVs:

- `transactions.csv` — what the platform recorded. Fields: transaction_id, date, amount, type (payment/refund), merchant_id, customer_id
- `settlements.csv` — what the bank reported. Fields: settlement_id, transaction_id (reference back), settlement_date, amount, status

The transaction_id is the join key. When things go wrong, that link breaks — missing references, amount mismatches, or timing gaps across month boundaries.

**Me:** What about the matching logic? How do I actually reconcile these?

**AI:** I'd do it in layers:

1. **Direct match** — join on transaction_id where amounts are equal. These are clean, mark them reconciled.
2. **Amount mismatches** — same transaction_id but amounts differ (rounding issues). Flag the delta.
3. **Unmatched transactions** — platform has it, bank doesn't. Check if it settled next month.
4. **Unmatched settlements** — bank has it, platform doesn't. These are your orphan refunds/chargebacks.
5. **Duplicate detection** — group by transaction_id and flag any ID appearing more than once in either dataset.

Then aggregate: total matched, total gaps, gap by category, and the actual dollar variance.

**Me:** What stack should I use?

**AI:** For a 2-hour assessment I'd go lean:

- **Python** for data generation and reconciliation logic — pandas makes the matching trivial
- **Flask** for a simple API to serve the results
- **HTML/CSS/JS** for the dashboard — just needs to show a summary + tables of discrepancies
- Deploy on **Render** or **Railway** for simplicity — single service, no frontend/backend split to manage

**Me:** Let me think about assumptions I need to state...

**AI:** Good call. Here are reasonable ones:

- Each transaction has a unique ID generated by the platform
- The bank references this ID when settling (in reality banks use their own refs, but for this exercise a shared ID is reasonable)
- Settlement happens 1-2 business days after the transaction
- All amounts are in a single currency (USD)
- Refunds are recorded as negative amounts in the bank feed
- Month-end cutoff is midnight UTC on the last day of the month
- No partial settlements — a transaction settles in full or not at all

**Me:** What would this get wrong in production?

**AI:** A few things:

- Real banks don't use the platform's transaction ID — you'd need fuzzy matching on amount + date + merchant, which is way harder and produces false positives.
- We're assuming a single currency. Multi-currency reconciliation adds FX rate variance as another gap type.
- In production, you'd deal with partial settlements, batch netting, and adjustments that span weeks — our 1-2 day settlement window is a simplification.
